## 3. Transformations of other heterocycles

The strategy of transformation of one heterocycle into another is a potent and convenient means for the construction of a large diversity of heterocyclic structures. 89

The low resonance energy of the furan ring determines the ease of ring transformation giving various carbo- and heterocycles. 90 Of particular interest is ring transformation of easily accessible furan derivatives to indoles. An enormous role in the development of studies on the conversion of furans into indoles belongs to Professor A.V. Butin and his colleagues and followers.

Thus, the acid-catalyzed one-pot synthesis of indoles from o-aminobenzyl alcohols and furans has been developed (Scheme 56). 91 The method includes the in situ formation of aminobenzylfurans A and the subsequent ring transformation to afford indole 60 containing an ethyl ketone group in position 2. The method is scalable and tolerates a broad range of substrates and more functional groups than the two-step method developed previously. 92

The presence of this functional group in the indole bicyclic moiety implies the possibility of further transformations of indole into 2,3- and 1,2-fused indole derivatives and into indoles containing an a,b-unsaturated carbonyl moiety. The reduction of the carbonyl group of indole 60b (R1 = H, R2 = R3 = Ph) and the subsequent Friedel-Crafts cyclization give rise to tetracyclic indole 61. Recently, an unusual domino reaction has been proposed in which the same a-carbon atom of furan reacts, first, as a nucleophile and then as an electrophile. In the presence of acids, N-tosylfurfurylamine (66) reacts with 2-(tosylamino)benzyl alcohol giving rise to 2-(acylvinyl)indoles 67a-n (Scheme 58, Table 6). 94 The reaction proceeds as the Friedel-Crafts alkylation of furan position 2 followed by acid-catalyzed intramolecular attack by the ortho-amino group on the same atom.

The process optimization is mainly related to the choice of the acid. Three acidic agents, namely, AcOH-HCl (method a), CF3CO2H (method b) and CuSO4·5H2O (method c), were tested with a broad range of substrates.

Method a resulted in the lowest yields of indoles 67e, f, l. The use of primary benzyl alcohol gave a moderate yield of indole 67n. This can be attributed to lower stability of primary carbocations, which leads to oligomerization and, as a consequence, to resinification of the reaction mixture. The presence of electron-withdrawing substituents in the benzene ring in the para-position relative to the amino group substantially decreases the yields of indoles (products 67i, j).
